Reading the data · health implications

What the standards actually mean.

Thresholds without context are abstractions. The following expands each indicator with what the published health evidence says — short-term effects, chronic risks, and the Bali-specific factors that change how to read these numbers.

PM2.5 · fine particulate
Why this specific number matters

Particles smaller than 2.5 micrometres — about one-thirtieth the width of a human hair — bypass the nose and throat, enter the deep lung, and cross into the bloodstream. Long-term exposure is associated with heart disease, stroke, chronic lung disease, lung cancer, and measurable reductions in life expectancy. Children, older adults, and people with cardiovascular or respiratory conditions are disproportionately affected.

Mixed-waste burning — particularly of plastics — emits PM2.5 alongside dioxins, furans, polycyclic aromatic hydrocarbons, and heavy metals. Standard low-cost PM sensors capture only the particulate portion. The toxic organic compounds released in the same plume are not measured by the instruments behind this dashboard, and their absence from the record should not be read as their absence from the air.

WHO revised guidelines downward in 2021 to reflect updated evidence of harm at lower concentrations than previously thought safe. The annual target of 5 µg/m³ is what protects you over a lifetime; the 24-hour target of 15 µg/m³ protects you on individual days. Most populated Bali stations exceed the 24-hour guideline routinely.

Climate · disease vectors
The mosquito-weather link

Aedes aegypti, the mosquito that transmits dengue, Zika, chikungunya, and yellow fever, breeds fastest in warm humid conditions. Optimal: 26–30 °C with relative humidity above 70%. Bali sits inside this window for most of the year.

The relevant indoor factors are humidity (because eggs survive better in damp microclimates), temperature stability (warmth across the day rather than just peaks), and standing water in or near the home. Sensors can tell you the first two; only people can report the third.

Dengue is endemic in Bali and outbreaks track rainy-season humidity. The WHO/CDC guidance for resident communities is straightforward: keep indoor humidity below 60% where possible, empty containers that collect water, use repellent at dawn and dusk when Aedes bites. Higher-humidity weeks are when mosquito-control campaigns matter most.

Noise · sleep and cardiovascular
What you don't hear still matters

The WHO Environmental Noise Guidelines (2018) recommend night-time exposure stay below 30 dB indoor and 40 dB outdoor — the levels where sleep architecture remains intact. Chronic exposure above these has measurable cardiovascular effects, even when the sleeper does not consciously notice the noise.

The metric that matters for residential health is Lnight — the night-time average from 22:00 to 06:00 — not peak events. A single 80 dB scooter at 11pm is less harmful than a sustained 42 dB hum throughout the night.

For workspaces, the EPA daytime intrusion threshold of 55 dB and the NIOSH occupational ceiling of 85 dB are the relevant references. Above 85 dB sustained, hearing damage accumulates within hours.

Heat · tropical context
Why WHO indoor comfort doesn't fit Bali

WHO's 18–24 °C indoor comfort guideline reflects temperate-climate housing with central heating. In tropical climates, 27–30 °C is the normal acceptable range with adequate air movement, and the body adapts to it within weeks of arrival.

The real heat-stress risk in Bali is sustained outdoor exposure above 33 °C with high humidity — the WBGT (Wet Bulb Globe Temperature) at which the body can no longer cool itself effectively through sweat. Outdoor workers, construction sites, and rice-field labor are the populations most exposed. Heat days compound through harvest season and are projected to increase under climate scenarios.

Indoor humidity above 60% in the tropics also creates conditions for mold growth, dust-mite proliferation, and respiratory irritation. A small dehumidifier in bedrooms during peak monsoon weeks is the simplest intervention.

Methodology · Making Sense framework

How this campaign is built.

The campaign follows the EU Making Sense participatory methodology (Fab Lab Barcelona / IAAC, 2015–2017), adapted by Fab Lab Bali for the Indonesian context. We're currently in Phase 1.

1 · ScopingCollect matters of concern from the community. Now.
2 · CommunityConvene contributors — schools, banjar, NGOs, fab labs.
3 · PlanningCo-design what to measure, where, how often.
4 · SensingDeploy sensors and the citizen-reporting bot.
5+ · Awareness · Action · ReflectionRead together, decide responses, document for the next bioregion.
