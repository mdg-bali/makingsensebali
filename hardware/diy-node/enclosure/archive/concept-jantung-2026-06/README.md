## The arc, honestly

- **Gen1** (sphere-sector bracts): bracts floated off the body like loose
  petals — a sphere can't hug a teardrop. Never shown.
- **Gen2** (body-surface bracts, tiny standoffs): bracts hugged invisibly;
  the form read as a smooth egg with engraved seams. Never shown.
- **Gen3** (legible standoffs 3/7/11mm): the imbrication finally read.
  Shown as A/B/C.
- **Gen4** (hybrid D): Tomas's recipe — B bottom + A crown, top tier lifted.
  Approved as a form language, then superseded by the batik/meru pivot
  before becoming the Phase-1 base.

## What this arc taught (carried into the next direction)

1. Surface elements must derive from the body surface itself, not from
   foreign primitives (sphere sectors) — else they float.
2. Offsets must be LEGIBLE: under ~2mm of standoff disappears at object
   scale; 3–11mm reads.
3. Tips/edges that part from the body are where the form gets its life —
   and where rain-shadowed vents naturally live.
4. The flat-back 270° wrap, ≤140mm envelope, bayonet service, and the
   imbricated-shingle rain logic all survive the pivot — they were
   engineering, not botany.

Engineering gates (coupons, slicer-in-loop, section prints) were never
reached by this arc: it ended at massing, exactly where cheap concepts
should end when the direction changes.
