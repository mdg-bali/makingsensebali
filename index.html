What these numbers mean

The reading, translated.

Numbers from sensors are abstract. The bands below ground them in everyday experience and known health thresholds. Where readings touch each band, that's what's happening in your air, your home, your sleep.

Fine particles your lungs can't filter
Smaller than 2.5 micrometres — they bypass your nose, enter the deep lung, and cross into the bloodstream. Long-term exposure is linked to heart disease, stroke, and reduced life expectancy. Burning waste releases particularly toxic versions.
0–5 µg/m³ · WHO annual target · forest after rain
5–15 · within WHO 24h · normal urban background
15–35 · above WHO 24h · roadside in light traffic
35–55 · sensitive groups affected · near a fire
55+ · unhealthy for everyone · cooking smoke indoors
Climate · disease vectors
Loading…
—
Mosquitoes love what Bali's air already is
Aedes aegypti — the dengue, Zika and chikungunya vector — breeds fastest at 26–30°C with humidity above 70%. Bali's tropical baseline puts most of the year inside that window. Indoor conditions matter as much as outdoor: standing water, damp corners, kitchen humidity.
<60% RH · breeding declines significantly
60–70% RH · moderate risk · empty standing water
70–80% RH + 26–30°C · high breeding conditions
>80% RH · also mould and dust mite problem

Quiet has a number
WHO 2018 night-time guidelines: 30 dB indoor / 40 dB outdoor. Chronic night exposure above 40 dB is linked to cardiovascular disease and disrupted sleep architecture. Even when you don't hear it, your body does.
30 dB · whisper, library
50 dB · refrigerator hum, light rain
70 dB · vacuum cleaner, busy street

Heat · the tropical context
Loading…
—
Bali isn't temperate Europe
WHO's 18–24°C indoor comfort range was written for temperate climates. Bali's 27–30°C is normal with air movement. The real risk is sustained 33+ °C with high humidity — outdoor workers face heat-stress days that compound through harvest and construction season.
22–26 °C · ASHRAE work comfort
26–30 °C · tropical normal · acceptable with ventilation
30–33 °C · outdoor exertion gets harder
33+ °C with >70% RH · heat-stress for outdoor workers
How this works

Listen. Show. Act.

A simple three-step loop. We collect data, we make it visible, the community acts on what it sees.

01 · LISTEN
Collect from two sources
Open-hardware sensors across Bali measure air, noise, temperature, humidity — continuously. And an AI-assisted reporting bot on Telegram (Phase 2) lets residents send what they see — burning trash, smoke, water, noise — in their own words.
02 · SHOW
Make it visible
Both streams land on this public map. Every reading is compared against international health guidelines — WHO for air and noise, EPA and ASHRAE for indoor environments. Translated into plain language. No login, no tracking, no paywall.
03 · ACT
Awareness → action
Visibility is the start. From there: community workshops, advocacy with banjar and local government, behavior change in homes and schools. Fab Lab Bali coordinates the response loop with local partners.

Phase 1 (now): survey of matters of concern · Phase 2: reporting bot · Phase 3: action with local partners

Who leads · where the methodology comes from

Local lead, credited methodology.

Fab Lab Bali leads the campaign — local, accountable, run by people who live here. The methodology comes from the EU Making Sense project (2015–2017, Fab Lab Barcelona / IAAC). Bali is its own place; the community here is the co-author of what this becomes.

Take the survey

Tell us what matters.

Five minutes. Anonymous if you want. No technical knowledge needed. Your answers shape what we measure, in which language, and who the data goes to.

→ Open the survey

What the survey asks

Eight questions.

  1. What environmental issue do you most want made visible?
  2. Where? (neighborhood, road, school, beach)
  3. Who is most affected?
  4. What evidence already exists?
  5. What would change if this were visible — and to whom?
  6. Want to take part further? (host a sensor, report, translate, attend workshops)
  7. Preferred languages.
  8. Optional contact.
